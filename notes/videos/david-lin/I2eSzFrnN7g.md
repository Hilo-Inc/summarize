# 'Late Stage Bull Market'; Trader Reveals Next Asset To Fall 40% | Gareth Soloway

### Late-Stage Bull Market: Reading the Warning Signs

Gareth Soloway, chief market strategist at Verified Investing, opens by drawing a distinction between the headline index performance and the broader market reality. The S&P 500 is at new all-time highs and the NASDAQ has just pierced 25,000, yet the expanded tech software ETF (IGF) is down roughly 20% year to date as AI developments render portions of the software sector obsolete. Meta and Microsoft have been crushed relative to expectations. Soloway says this pattern -- a handful of mega-cap names propping up the indexes while most of the market lags -- is the defining signature of a late-stage bull market, explicitly analogous to the dot-com era of 1999-2000 when Amazon and a few other names were doing the heavy lifting while hundreds of stocks were already quietly deteriorating.
He points to a specific historical parallel: in 2000 the NASDAQ pierced 5,000 by about 150 points before topping out. The index has now crossed 25,000. He is not calling an immediate collapse, but he is calling for caution and watching closely whether the index can hold above current levels for at least a week to confirm that the breakout is real rather than a false move.

### The Engine Keeping the Economy Running

The primary driver sustaining both the economy and the stock market, in Soloway's view, is AI-related capital expenditure from the largest technology companies. He estimates the big players are collectively spending around $700 billion per year on data centers, infrastructure, semiconductors, and AI buildout -- a stimulus effect comparable in scale to COVID-era government spending. Jerome Powell himself acknowledged at the most recent FOMC press conference that data centers and AI capex have been major drivers of economic activity, a point Soloway treats as validation of his framework.
The consumer picture, however, is sharply bifurcated. Soloway describes a K-shaped economy in which roughly 70% of Americans -- particularly the bottom half of the income distribution -- feel they are already in a recession. They are being squeezed by elevated prices and have limited exposure to financial assets. Meanwhile, asset owners are thriving on the back of rising stock prices and continuing to spend freely. This upper-tier spending is keeping the engine running, but Soloway warns it is fragile: once the high-end consumer pulls back, there is nothing else to fill the void, and the contraction tends to be fast.
*"As long as the US economy is hanging in there and as long as earnings are strong, that's all the market cares about."*
He extends this logic to the oil situation. Despite crude oil rising sharply and geopolitical tension in the Middle East, markets have continued to climb because US earnings data has remained solid. The market, he argues, has blinders on and is ignoring the lagging effects of high energy costs that will eventually filter through into producer prices, consumer budgets, and ultimately earnings themselves.

### Recession Timing: 2027, Not 2026

Soloway puts the probability of a technical recession in 2026 at very low, broadly consistent with prediction market pricing. His reasoning is that as long as Meta, Amazon, Google, and Microsoft are each spending $150-200 billion annually on AI infrastructure, that spending acts as a powerful economic buffer that prevents the contraction from becoming broad enough to register as a recession in the official data. Many parts of the economy, he acknowledges, are already contracting -- regional banks, lower-income consumers, portions of manufacturing -- but the aggregate GDP figure is being held up by the scale of tech investment.
The real recession trigger, in his view, will be when the mega-caps start pulling back their capex. If each of the big four reduces annual spend from $150-200 billion to $100 billion, the aggregate withdrawal from the economy is enormous, and that is when a genuine recession hits. He now puts that more realistically in the 2027 timeframe, having previously forecast it for late 2026. He explicitly acknowledges this is a revision from his view a few months earlier when he was more confident about a year-end recession. He attributes the shift largely to the surprising resilience of AI capex spending, which he had underweighted.

### Inflation: A Transitory Spike on Top of a Structural Problem

On inflation, Soloway separates two distinct dynamics. The first is the current oil-driven spike, which he describes as potentially transitory in the classic sense -- once crude oil comes back down to $60-70 a barrel (which he thinks is likely, in part because of political pressure ahead of the midterms), the headline inflation number will ease. The three-month duration of the current oil spike does mean producers have had to absorb elevated input costs long enough that some of that will pass through to final prices, so the relief will not be immediate or complete.
The second and more persistent problem is structural inflation driven by government fiscal policy. Inflation came down from its 2022 peak but never reached the Fed's 2% target, stabilizing instead around 2.7% and now edging higher. Soloway argues that with the federal government running roughly a trillion dollars of additional debt per quarter, the long-run trajectory for money supply growth points toward inflation that stays structurally elevated -- probably in the 3-4% range -- for years. For average Americans already under pressure, 3-4% annual price increases compounding across every category of spending is, in his words, unsustainable, but he sees no political will to meaningfully cut spending, so the pressure remains.
He frames 2026 as a year in which inflation hedging makes sense as a theme, though he qualifies it carefully: hedge for the structural component, but do not extrapolate the oil-driven spike as permanent.

### Federal Reserve Dynamics and the Powell Succession Question

The FOMC held rates unchanged at its most recent meeting, but the meeting was notable for producing four dissents -- the most since 1992. Soloway focuses on the nature of those dissents. Three of the four came not from governors opposed to holding rates steady, but from governors who objected to any language in Powell's statement that could be read as hinting at future easing. These were hawkish dissents, signaling that significant portions of the committee are not comfortable even telegraphing a dovish pivot.
Soloway reads this as strategically important in the context of Jerome Powell's announced plan to stay on as a governor after his chairmanship ends, with Kevin Warsh widely expected to become the next chair. Powell's continued presence as a governor, Soloway argues, provides a structural anchor for Fed independence, because Warsh would face a voting bloc of governors already on record as hawkish. The chairman does not unilaterally set policy -- votes do -- and the composition of the current board makes it harder for any incoming chair to simply accommodate political pressure for rate cuts. Soloway sees this as a market positive, since the market craves predictability and institutional independence from the Fed above almost anything else.

### S&P 500 Technical Outlook: Watching the Parallel Channel

Soloway has been tracking a long-term parallel channel on the S&P 500 drawn from the COVID lows. The channel has repeatedly provided reliable support at the lower bound and resistance at the upper bound, with the midpoint also functioning as a significant technical level. During the most recent pullback -- triggered in part by the onset of the Iran conflict -- the index dropped precisely to the channel midpoint, which is where Soloway says he began going long with members at Verified Investing, a call he describes as "buying with both hands."
The subsequent rally has been extraordinary by any historical measure: the NASDAQ has gained roughly 21-22% from its late-March lows in approximately one month. Soloway says he did not expect that magnitude of move and has already exited around 99% of his long positions. The index is now pressing against the upper band of the parallel channel, and he is beginning to build short positions, legging in incrementally rather than committing a full position at once. His process is to add a quarter position, then if the market continues higher, add another quarter, building a core short gradually.
*"I'm not always right -- I must be open to the possibility that it could get through and continue up if it shows itself that it can."*
His near-term test is simple: can the S&P and NASDAQ hold above current levels for at least a week? If not, and the index closes back inside the channel, he believes a major top may be in the making. His first downside target on a decline would be prior all-time highs, which have now become technical support. Below that, he would look for a move back toward the channel midpoint. He uses former all-time high levels as sequential targets, intending to cover portions of his short at each support zone and reshort bounces.
He offers the NASDAQ-to-M2 money supply ratio as one of his most striking valuation arguments. When the NASDAQ's price level is divided by M2 money supply, the ratio is currently slightly above where it was at the dot-com peak. This is a rebuttal to the common argument that abundant market liquidity makes a major decline impossible: in real money-supply-adjusted terms, the market is not cheap relative to 2000.

### Intel as a Case Study in Irrational Exuberance

Soloway highlights Intel as a specific example of late-cycle excess. The stock, which was trading at levels that prompted serious questions about its long-term viability roughly 18 months ago, has since surged more than 150% from its March lows and recently crossed $100 per share, giving it a market capitalization above $500 billion. It is now trading at a forward price-to-earnings ratio of 80-90 by his estimate. He attributes the reversal largely to government backing and a favorable regulatory posture rather than any fundamental transformation of the business.
He draws an explicit parallel to the blowoff tops of the late 1990s, where stocks that were objectively overvalued continued to climb on momentum and retail enthusiasm before collapsing sharply. His interpretation is that Intel's move, impressive as it is, is a sentiment signal rather than a fundamental one -- the kind of move that appears at tops, not at the beginning of sustainable bull cycles.

### Micron: Bearish Again After Another Leg Higher

Soloway had previously called a short on Micron during a mid-March appearance, pointing to a double-top formation, a piercing of the upper parallel band, and a bearish RSI divergence -- a higher price high accompanied by a lower RSI high. Within two days of that call, Micron began falling and ultimately dropped around 33%. A subsequent announcement from Google about a new AI model requiring less memory provided a fundamental catalyst, but Soloway notes he had no advance knowledge of that announcement; the technical picture alone supported the trade.
Micron has since staged another significant rally and is now pressing against the upper boundary of its longer-term parallel channel again. Soloway says he is bearish on Micron again for the same reasons: the stock is hitting upper parallel resistance, the pattern of prior pivots suggests a rejection at this level, and the overall chart structure points toward a reversion to the lower end of the channel. His year-end target for Micron is around $350, which would represent a roughly $200 decline from current levels. He describes the current parallel on Micron as one of the cleaner and more reliable channel structures he has seen.

### Gold: Structurally Bullish, Near-Term Neutral

Gold has already pulled back significantly from its highs, and Soloway describes himself as currently neutral on it as a swing trade while maintaining long-term holdings. He is not actively shorting gold because the easy downside move has already occurred, and the risk-reward for a fresh short is unattractive from current levels. His framework requires either a better entry point or a clearer signal.
His key technical levels are $3,900 on the downside -- the first major support zone and the lower end of the channel -- and $5,000 on the upside, which would constitute a breakout and likely lead to a retest of all-time highs and potentially beyond. The current price sits roughly in the middle of those two levels, which is precisely why he is neutral on it for short-term trading purposes.
The caveat he adds is that if the stock market undergoes a more severe correction -- something on the order of a 20% drawdown in the NASDAQ -- gold could be dragged down to the $3,500 area as risk-off selling hits all asset classes indiscriminately. He views gold as having morphed into a risk asset in the current cycle, driven by a wave of buyers who treated it as an easy momentum trade rather than as a genuine safe-haven holding. He argues gold will not make its next real bull move until those buyers have been flushed out and only core, long-term holders remain.
He is emphatic that his long-term view on gold remains bullish. Until he sees meaningful fiscal discipline from the US government, he intends to use every significant pullback as a buying opportunity for long-term holdings. Silver falls into the same category. The 10-year Treasury yield and gold price show a close inverse relationship at present: if bond vigilantes push yields higher in the absence of Fed action, that creates a headwind for gold that could persist. The bond market, he notes, is sending a cautionary signal -- the 10-year yield is hanging near 4.5% -- even as equities act as if all risks have been resolved.

### Bitcoin: Bearish Flag, $50,000 Target

Bitcoin, in Soloway's read, is now tracing out a classic bear flag. The pattern consists of a sharp decline followed by a period of sideways consolidation with a slight upward tilt, which statistically resolves to the downside more often than not. He can identify an almost identical pattern earlier in Bitcoin's recent history that played out in exactly this way. The current consolidation has lasted longer than the prior instance, which he attributes largely to the strength of the stock market rally providing a backdrop of general risk appetite.
His specific levels: as long as Bitcoin stays below $85,000, he is bearish. If it were to break decisively above $85,000, there would be a potential breakout trade toward $100,000. But his base case is that it stays capped and eventually breaks lower, with a downside target of approximately $50,000 -- a decline of roughly 37-38% from current levels around $80,000.
He points to the divergence between Bitcoin and gold as significant. Over the period when gold was making a major run, Bitcoin was actually declining. In prior cycles, Bitcoin tracked risk assets with high correlation, but in the current environment it has underperformed both equities and gold. He attributes this in part to the proliferation of alternative tokens and what he describes as actions by the administration that, despite initial bullish expectations from the crypto community, were not ultimately constructive for the broader crypto ecosystem. He specifically mentions the launch of multiple new coins and actions that he describes as "rug-pull-ish" in character, which damaged confidence in the sector broadly.
There is also a rotation argument: capital that might previously have flowed to Bitcoin as a speculative vehicle is instead going into semiconductors and AI-adjacent equities, which offer similarly dramatic return profiles with more immediate fundamental narratives. Soloway acknowledges the contrarian case -- sentiment among Bitcoin holders is genuinely poor and historically that has marked important bottoms -- but says he cannot act on that until he sees actual price behavior support the thesis. Historically, when the NASDAQ drops 10-15%, Bitcoin almost always declines as well rather than attracting safe-haven flows, so a rotation of capital from equities into Bitcoin remains a possibility rather than a documented pattern.
On the question of which falls first -- stocks or Bitcoin -- Soloway says he would expect the stock market to see the initial and larger move down, since Bitcoin has already discounted significantly from its own all-time highs. If forced to choose between being long Bitcoin or long the stock market at current prices, he says he would choose Bitcoin because it has already priced in more downside. The stock market, by contrast, is at or near all-time highs and carrying late-cycle valuation signals.

### Natural Gas: The One Long

The only asset where Soloway says he is actively adding risk on the long side is natural gas. He describes it as a short-term trade, watching for a breakout above the 288 level after a period of downtrend that appears to have been broken. His thesis involves several components: historically, when oil prices come in, capital has sometimes rotated into natural gas within the energy complex; data centers require enormous amounts of power and neither nuclear nor other clean energy sources are ready to meet that demand at scale; and natural gas remains significantly cheaper per unit of energy than oil, making it a logical candidate to serve as a primary power source for AI infrastructure buildout.
He is otherwise holding significant cash on the sidelines and running a modest short book on equities. His posture is to wait for a proper market correction before going back to building long positions in gold, silver, and selectively in equities. He characterizes the current moment as one requiring patience rather than aggression -- the bull market signals are there in the headline index, but the late-stage characteristics argue against committing new capital on the long side at current levels.
