## I Built a $20K/Month App in 83 Days

Pat Walls interviews **Brian Shin**, co-founder of **Once** — a digital disposable camera app for events. Brian and his girlfriend launched in December 2025, hit **$20K MRR in 83 days**, and the entire growth playbook hinges on a single pre-build idea: the **commitment metric**.

### The App: Once

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Launched | December 2025 |
| Time to $20K MRR | 83 days |
| Weekly active users | 10,000–12,000 |
| Current monthly revenue | ~$22,000 |
| Scheduled events (Feb) | 300+ |
| Scheduled events (Mar) | ~700 |
| Pricing — 10-guest party | $2 |
| Pricing — 150-guest wedding | $50 |

Pricing scales with guest count per event. Use cases: weddings, birthday parties, increasingly corporate events.

### The Idea

Brian and his girlfriend traveled 7–8 countries last year, bringing a physical disposable camera everywhere. The friction of the medium — no instant preview, limited shots — is what made the experience feel "real." Once is a digital recreation of those constraints.

> "Those limitations actually make the whole experience much more real. The idea behind Once was: why don't we build a digital experience that replicates the traditional disposable camera?"

### Background

- Previously co-founded a venture-backed, sales-driven B2B startup (~50 people).
- Discovered the bootstrap/indie-hacker scene while at the VC-backed company.
- Wanted full control of the product this time → fully bootstrapped with his girlfriend as co-founder.

### The Validation Playbook (the core of the video)

Step-by-step framework Brian would re-run if starting today:

#### Step 1 — Define your commitment metric

Two sides to it:

- **Commitment to yourself**: a specific date + specific number of customers/events/users you must hit. Bounds your runway.
- **Commitment from users**: doesn't have to be payment. For Once it was *event hosts agreeing to use Once at a real, dated event*. Pick the metric that genuinely signals usage for your vertical.

#### Step 2 — Exhaust your personal network

Open X, LinkedIn, Instagram/Facebook side-by-side. Walk every friend, circle anyone with a potential event coming up.

> Result for Brian: 4 friends said yes — a Halloween party, birthday, wedding, networking event.

Apply the **Mom Test**: be honest with yourself. You're validating, not collecting encouragement.

#### Step 3 — Build a scrappy mockup (max 2–3 days)

Figma or AI tools. Doesn't need to be polished. Just enough to show users a tangible thing.

#### Step 4 — Go where your users live

Pick the channel (Reddit, TikTok, YouTube, Instagram) and learn its ins and outs. Cold-message hard.

> "If you haven't been banned on these platforms at least two times, you haven't tried enough."

Brian searched Instagram `#wedding #birthdayparty`, listed 250–300 people, wrote a 2–3 sentence cold DM:

| Outreach | Count |
|----------|-------|
| People messaged | 250–300 |
| Replies | ~15 |
| Events booked (first month) | 12 |

That's when they decided to go all-in.

#### Step 5 — Set the number that triggers "go build"

For Once: **10 dated event commitments before writing a single line of production code.** A dated commitment to use the product at a real event with friends and loved ones present was treated as a proxy for payment.

### How the App Was Actually Built

- **First version**: web app, ~1–2 weeks, deployed for a friend's Halloween party. It broke repeatedly during the event but proved the core experience worked.
- **Then they threw the v1 away** and rebuilt it natively.
- **Design is never AI-assisted.** Consumer apps are a "craft" — design requires taste, and opinionated design produces better output.

### The AI-Era Trap (Pat & Gus's takeaway)

The hosts' meta-point at the end:

> "In a world where anyone can build anything in 1 hour, this is the secret sauce — setting your commitment metric of 'I need 10 people to say they're going to use this thing before I finish it.'"

- AI tools (Claude Code, etc.) make building so easy that founders skip the *hard, scary* part — cold-messaging strangers and old contacts.
- Pre-AI, you had to talk to people first because you *couldn't* build alone. That natural forcing function is gone.
- The new edge isn't engineering velocity. It's the discipline to *not* build until external commitment exists.

### Closing Advice

> "Stop overthinking and launch. Your assumptions may go to nothing once users start using it — you might be solving a completely different problem than you expected."
